Ground robotics · Eurobot / French Robotic Cup

A robot that reads the table and plans its own moves

With the ENSMASTEEL team, I built the software brain of an autonomous robot for Eurobot — it watches the playing field from above, decides a strategy, and carries out its tasks on its own during a timed match.

View on GitHub ↗ How it works

The goal

Eurobot is France's biggest student robotics competition. Two robots share a table and score points by completing tasks faster and smarter than the opponent — with no remote control. Everything has to be decided and executed by the robot itself within the match.

My job was the part that makes that autonomy possible: turning what a camera sees into a live understanding of the game, and turning that understanding into the right action at the right moment.

How it works

An overhead camera watches the table. From there, the software runs a clear loop:

See
Overhead camera
Locate
ArUco markers
Map
Live world model
Decide
Strategy brain
Act
Robot moves

Under the hood, the system is split into three clean layers, each with one job — a design choice that keeps the whole thing reliable and easy to test:

Layer 1
Brain (Jetson): sees the table, builds the world model, and decides the strategy.
Layer 2
Dispatcher (Raspberry Pi): a pure messenger that passes orders down and status back up — no decisions of its own.
Layer 3
Boards (motors, actuators, LiDAR): carry out the physical movement and sensing.

The clever bit: I also built a full simulator that replays the exact same strategy code with no hardware at all. That meant the team could develop and test the robot's decisions on a laptop, long before the real robot was ready.

My role

I designed and implemented the perception-to-decision architecture: the vision and localization, the strategy brain, the communication between layers, and the hardware-in-the-loop simulator that let us validate everything offline.

The honest part

This project was challenging and, at times, genuinely frustrating. Despite months of design and integration, a critical hardware failure consumed our competition days and stopped us from competing as planned.

It was a hard but formative lesson: the best software means nothing if the hardware under it fails. It pushed me to take robustness, redundancy and system integration as seriously as the algorithms themselves — a mindset I now bring to everything I build.
